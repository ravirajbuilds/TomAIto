The problem

The hidden cost of guessing when to pick.

Small apple and cherry growers lose income at both ends. Fruit picked too early grades low; disease caught too late spreads. Today's tools are slow, destructive, or out of reach.

🧪

Manual sampling

You walk the block, crush a few samples, and wait on lab Brix. It's slow, and it still only covers a fraction of the orchard.

🍇

Destructive testing

A refractometer means squeezing the fruit. Every ground-truth reading is a piece of sellable produce you just destroyed.

🦠

Disease caught late

Scab, mildew and rot are cheap to stop early and ruinous once they spread. The eye misses the first signs on a busy day.

How it works

Two sensors in. One verdict out. Under three seconds.

The shift

Ripeness and disease belong in the field, not the lab.

Powered by two sensors

A $3,000 lab capability in a $100 phone attachment.

🦠

On-device disease vision

A quantized CV model classifies visible disease (or "healthy"), plus a crop-health score from greenness, lesion and chlorosis signals. No connection needed.

🔬

NIR ripeness & Brix

An 18-channel spectral sensor (410–940 nm) reads internal quality the camera can't see, estimating ripeness stage and sugar in °Brix.

🔀

One fused verdict, spoken

Surface and internal readings combine into a single 🟢/🟠/🔴 call with plain-language advice, read aloud with weather-aware guidance, hands-free.

📈

Trends that learn your block

Tag a plant or block and re-scan across the season to watch ripening curves and disease risk. Confirm or correct each call to train it for your orchard.

Honest by design

Rigor is the point.

The AS7265x tops out near 940 nm, which is great for ripeness, chlorophyll and surface chemistry. We don't claim deep 1450 nm sugar/water bands that need far pricier sensors. Here are the v1 targets we hold ourselves to.
