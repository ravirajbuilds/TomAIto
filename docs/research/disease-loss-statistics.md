## 1. General Framing — Global & US Crop Loss to Pests and Disease

### Global crop loss
- Plant pests and diseases destroy **up to 40% of global crop production every year** (FAO / IPPC, 2021).
- Plant diseases cost the global economy **over $220 billion annually**; invasive insects cost **at least $70 billion annually** (FAO / IPPC, 2021).
- Peer-reviewed crop-specific yield losses to the full pathogen+pest complex — the strongest academic anchor for the "~20–40%" framing — from **Savary et al., *Nature Ecology & Evolution*, 2019** (137 pathogens/pests, five staple crops; mean with range):
  - Wheat **21.5%** (10.1–28.1%) · Rice **30.0%** (24.6–40.9%) · Maize **22.5%** (19.5–41.1%) · Potato **17.2%** (8.1–21.0%) · Soybean **21.4%** (11.0–32.4%) (Savary et al., *Nat. Ecol. Evol.*, 2019).

### Postharvest loss (food, and fruits/vegetables specifically)
- About **one-third (~1.3 billion tonnes) of all food produced for human consumption is lost or wasted globally each year** (FAO, *Global Food Losses and Food Waste*, 2011).
- The rigorous farm-to-retail figure: **~14% of the world's food (≈$400 billion/yr) is lost between harvest and the retail stage** (FAO, *State of Food and Agriculture 2019*); North America & Europe lose **>15%** in this segment (FAO SOFA, 2019).
- **Fruits and vegetables are the highest-loss category.** FAO's SAVE FOOD work has used **~45% loss for fruits/vegetables** (FAO SAVE FOOD, ~2011–2019). In developing-region fresh-produce chains, losses run **30–50%** and higher (FAO; Postharvest Education Foundation, ~2022).
  - ⚠️ The popular shorthand "**~50% of fruits/vegetables are lost**" is defensible only as a *developing-region / whole-chain* figure, **not** a universal global number; the defensible global farm-to-retail figure is **~14%** (FAO SOFA, 2019). Treat "~50%" as an upper bound, clearly caveated.

### Cost of traditional diagnosis vs. value of early detection
- **University plant-diagnostic clinic fees (per sample)** — confirms the "$20–$75" range used in the pitch:
  - UW–Madison **~$20–25** routine · Univ. of Minnesota **~$50** (oak **$75–95**) · NDSU **≤$50** routine, **$80 + $40–45/sample** for molecular qPCR · UF/IFAS **$40** standard (Rapid Turfgrass **$75**) · Ohio State **$50+** out-of-state (respective university plant clinics, current, 2025–26).
- **Crop-consultant / field-scouting cost:** roughly **$5.75–$8.43 per acre** for season-long IPM scouting programs (industry survey via Spectrum Analytic / AgProfessional, 2005 — dated; order-of-magnitude). **An orchard-specific WA scouting rate per acre was not found. [unverified]**
- **Value of early detection (tree-fruit example):** fire blight (*Erwinia amylovora*) costs US growers **>$100 million/yr** in losses + control, and early detection/localization of infected trees is repeatedly cited as essential to timely, lower-cost containment (Virginia Tech Extension; MDPI *Horticulturae*, 2023–2024). Published reviews describe early detection as reducing treatment intensity, pesticide use, and labor versus fighting a full outbreak, but give **qualitative** rather than single-number savings (plant-pathogen detection reviews, PMC, 2023).

## 2. APPLE

### 2a. United States — industry scale
- **US apple production:** ~250 million bushels / >10.5 billion lb (2023/24); forecast ~**279 million bushels** (2025/26) (US Apple Association, 2023; 2025).
- **US farm-gate value ~$5 billion/yr**, with ~**$15 billion/yr** downstream (packing, shipping, processing) (American Farm Bureau Federation, *Market Intel*). ⚠️ The $5B farm-gate figure is an industry round number and exceeds the sum of major-state NASS production values (WA alone ~$1.95B); treat as **approximate / methodology-dependent**.
- Apples are one of the three largest US non-citrus fruit crops (USDA ERS, 2023).

### 2b. United States — diseases & losses
- **Apple scab (*Venturia inaequalis*)** — the **most economically damaging apple disease worldwide** in humid temperate regions. Yield loss **20–70% if uncontrolled** (up to ~100%), and **70%+ in cool, wet springs** (Bowen et al., *Mol. Plant Pathol.*, 2011; apple-scab critical review, 2018). Control requires **10–18+ fungicide sprays/yr** at roughly **$82–$205/acre** ($202–506/ha) (global apple-scab literature, 2018).
- **Fire blight (*Erwinia amylovora*)** — US annual losses + control **>$100 million/yr** (widely cited). The famous PNW figure: **>$68 million in apple+pear losses in Washington and northern Oregon**, attributed to **Stockwell et al., 2002**, and tied to the **1998** regional epidemic — *not* 2000 (verified via Frontiers/PMC fire-blight papers, 2015). ⚠️ The "**2000 outbreak**" framing conflates two events: the **$68M PNW loss was 1998**; the **2000 epidemic was in Michigan** (~**$42 million**, ~**350,000–450,000 trees / 1,550–2,300 acres removed**) (Michigan State / Longstroth, widely cited). Both are real; keep them distinct.
- **Powdery mildew (*Podosphaera leucotricha*)** — up to **50% production loss** if unmanaged; reduces fruit set, quality, vigor, and return bloom (APS *Plant Health Progress*, 2021).
- **Bitter rot (*Colletotrichum* spp.)** — a devastating *summer rot*, but concentrated in the **Eastern/Mid-Atlantic/Southeast US**: losses **14–100%** (NY 14–25%, NC up to 100%, KY ~30% avg) (APS journals; *Sci. Reports*, 2020).
- **Postharvest decay — blue mold (*Penicillium expansum*) & gray mold (*Botrytis cinerea*)**: blue mold is the most prevalent/virulent pome-fruit postharvest decay, causing **>50% of postharvest decay**, and *P. expansum* produces the mycotoxin patulin (a food-safety concern) (*Postharvest Biology & Technology*, 2024).
- **Postharvest loss in storage (apple):** decay is the leading cause, typically **~5–20% of losses** rule-of-thumb (Progressive Crop Consultant, 2020); real-world combined decay + physiological-disorder losses of **~18–27%** have been measured in long controlled-atmosphere storage + shelf life (Frontiers in Horticulture, 2025).

### 2c. Washington — industry scale (the apple capital)
- **Washington grows ~60–65% of the US apple crop** ("60–70%" range **verified**: NWHC cites ~65%, and the PNW as a whole = ~70% of total / 78% of *fresh* US apples) (Northwest Horticultural Council). WA has been the #1 apple state since the 1920s and grows **>90% of US organic apples** (NWHC).
- **Value of utilized production (USDA NASS):** **$1.95 billion (2024)**, up from prior year; **$1.99 billion (2023)** — apples are **Washington's #1 agricultural commodity** (~16% of state farm-gate value), within a **~$14 billion** total state ag economy in 2023 (USDA NASS, 2024–2025). Industry forecasts for the record 2025/26 crop reach **~$2.3 billion** at >180–190 million bushels (Washington State Tree Fruit Association, 2025) — *forecast, partially* **[unverified in full]**.
- **Footprint:** ~**150,000–174,000 bearing acres**; ~**134 million** 40-lb fresh boxes (2023 crop); nearly **30% exported** (USDA / WSTFA, 2023–2024).
- **Economic impact & jobs:** ~**$7.5 billion** total annual economic impact to the state; nearly **40,000 direct + ~21,000 indirect jobs** in WA (WA+OR combined **68,400**) (Good Fruit Grower; Northwest Horticultural Council).

### 2d. Washington — which diseases actually matter here (key for OrchardEye)
- **High priority in central WA:**
  - **Fire blight** — best-documented WA per-acre costs: in the **2017–2018** WA epidemics growers spent **~$250/acre on preventative sprays** and **$27–$864/acre removing cankered wood**; a **10% rootstock-blight incidence in a 4-yr-old orchard can cause losses up to $3,500/acre** (WSU Tree Fruit, *Fire Blight of Apple and Pear*).
  - **Powdery mildew** — explicitly a threat "in the semiarid regions of central Washington State where ~66% of total US apple fruits are produced" (APS mildew literature, 2021). A WA-specific $/acre management cost was **not found. [unverified]**
  - **Postharvest decay in WA storage:** of decayed stored WA apples, **blue mold (Penicillium) 32%, gray mold (Botrytis) 28%, Sphaeropsis rot 17%** of decayed fruit (Kim & Xiao, *Plant Disease*, APS, 2008).
- **Lower priority in WA (climate-limited — note as "watch items"):**
  - **Apple scab** — suppressed by arid central-WA climate ("scab risk is low under arid conditions in Central Washington") (WSU Tree Fruit / PNW Pest Management Handbooks). Top-tier globally, secondary here.
  - **Bitter rot** — "so few reports… that it is absent from regional lists of apple diseases" in the semiarid West (WSU 2019, cited in APS *Phytopathology*, 2020). A national/eastern problem expanding with warming.
- **Replant disease (soilborne, on replanting):** can cost **$70,000–$150,000/acre** in lost productivity over a new planting's first four years — an establishment loss, not annual (WSU Tree Fruit, *Apple Replant Disease*).
- **Consolidated total disease-management $/acre for a typical WA orchard:** not found as a single citable number; see WSU's **2024 Apple Costs and Returns Production Studies** for cost-center breakdowns. **[unverified]**

## 3. SWEET CHERRY

### 3a. Industry scale (US & Washington)
- **Washington is the dominant US sweet cherry producer**, reaching **~62% of US production by 2023** (with CA ~32%); WA + CA + OR = **>90% of US output** (farmdoc daily, Univ. of Illinois, 2024).
- **Washington value of production:** **$290 million (2023)** (USDA NASS, 2023); a later NASS/Capital Press figure put the **2024** WA crop at **~$435 million** on ~200,970 tons (prices rose sharply year-over-year) (USDA NASS via Capital Press, 2025). *Both are valid for their stated years; the jump reflects price, not just volume.*
- **Footprint:** ~**40,000 bearing acres** in WA (USDA NASS, 2025). US total sweet cherry value ~**$817 million (2024)** — so WA ≈ half of US value (USDA NASS via Capital Press, 2025).

### 3b. Little Cherry Disease / X-Disease complex — the headline threat ⭐
**This is the best-documented, most economically severe cherry disease story in WA — and the strongest motivating case for OrchardEye.**

**Causal agents (three distinct pathogens, one symptom complex):**
- **Little cherry virus 1 (LChV1)**, **Little cherry virus 2 (LChV2)**, and the **X-disease phytoplasma (Western X), *Candidatus* Phytoplasma pruni** (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024). In WA, **LChV2 and the X-disease phytoplasma** are the major causes; **Western X is a phytoplasma, not a virus** — which matters for diagnosis (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024).

**Documented economic losses (peer-reviewed; WSU + OSU grower survey — Molnar, DuPont & Thompson, *Journal of Extension*, 2022):**
- **238,856 sweet cherry trees removed = ~974 acres** pushed out due to X-disease / little cherry disease, **2015–2020** (Molnar et al., *J. Extension*, 2022; WSU Tree Fruit).
- Plus **33,082 peach/nectarine/plum/apricot trees (~81 acres)** removed — the same phytoplasma infects other stone fruit (Molnar et al., 2022).
- **Revenue reduction: ~$30 million in 2020 alone** and **~$65 million cumulatively, 2015–2020** (Molnar et al., 2022; WSU Tree Fruit).
- **Per-acre loss: an average $118,095 per acre** in establishment + lost revenue across the **seven-year** orchard re-establishment period — *the headline $/acre number* (Molnar et al., 2022; WSU Tree Fruit).
- **Total grower lost revenue + establishment costs over the re-establishment period: ~$115 million** (Molnar et al., 2022).
- Removals continued past the survey window: WSU ran a **cost-share program for little cherry / X-disease tree removal** into 2024 (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024; The Columbian, 2024).

**Why it is so damaging:**
- Produces **small, poorly colored, low-sugar (low-brix), bitter, unmarketable fruit** — a quality/marketability loss, not just yield (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024).
- **No cure.** The only remedy is removing infected trees + vector control (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024).
- **Vector spread:** X-disease phytoplasma by **leafhoppers**; LChV2 by **mealybugs** (apple mealybug, grape mealybug); also by grafting infected wood (WSU Tree Fruit; PNW Handbooks).
- **Latent/symptomless carriers** can take ~a season to show symptoms and are detectable only by **molecular (PCR/qPCR)** assays — silent spreaders that seed neighboring blocks (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024; improved LChV2 qPCR, *Plant Disease*, APS, 2022). *This latency is exactly the gap a fast visual/scanner screen helps close.*

### 3c. Other cherry diseases
- **Powdery mildew (*Podosphaera clandestina*)** — "one of the most serious diseases of cherries in the Pacific Northwest"; white colonies render fruit unmarketable (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024). Cherry-specific $ loss **not found. [unverified]**
- **Bacterial canker / gummosis (*Pseudomonas syringae*)** — especially damaging to young trees; **up to 75% tree loss / up to 60% incidence reported in young WA orchards in 2021** (after an April frost), and **~10–40% yield loss** in commercial orchards (WSU Tree Fruit, 2024).
- **Brown rot (*Monilinia* spp.)** — explosive pre- and postharvest rot under wet conditions; mature fruit can decay within ~48 h (PNW Pest Management Handbooks). Cherry-specific PNW $ loss **not found. [unverified]**
- **Cherry leaf spot (*Blumeriella jaapii*)** — early-summer defoliation can depress bloom/fruit set for up to two following years; fewer infection cycles in the PNW than the Midwest (PNW Pest Management Handbooks). WA-specific $ figure **[unverified]**.
- **Postharvest decay (general)** — a generic ~9% US postharvest decay loss in transport/marketing is cited but is **not cherry-specific [unverified]**.

## 4. TOMATO (United States) — and why it's the ML prototyping crop

### 4a. US industry scale
- Tomatoes are among the **three highest-value US vegetable crops** (with romaine and carrots, those three = 34% of the **$19.5 billion** 2023 US vegetable value) (USDA NASS, *Vegetables 2023 Summary*, 2024).
- **Fresh-market** tomato production value ~**$715.6 million (2023)**, grown commercially in ~20 states (USDA/Statista; AgMRC). **Florida** (fresh-market leader): **897 million lb, ~$494 million (2023)** (USDA NASS).
- **Processing:** ~**248,000 acres @ 50 tons/acre (2023)**; **California** dominates at **12.9 million tons (2023)** with a negotiated price of **$138/ton** → roughly **$1.7–1.8 billion** contracted value (USDA NASS; California Tomato Growers Assn. via Farm Progress, 2023). Tomatoes are **>70%** of US processing-vegetable volume (USDA NASS, 2025).
- ⚠️ A single published **combined fresh + processing** national farm value for 2023 was **not found** (segments reported separately). **[unverified as a combined figure]**

### 4b. Major tomato diseases & impact
- **Late blight (*Phytophthora infestans*)** — globally **~$6.7 billion/yr** in combined tomato+potato losses + control (USDA ARS / USABlight). In the US, **2009** fresh-market and processing tomato industries saw **US$46M and US$66M** in total yield losses respectively, with up to half attributable to late blight; late blight ≈ **7% of US tomato yield loss** (Plant Disease / late-blight literature, 2009–2013). An unprotected crop can be destroyed in **7–10 days** (APS, 2013).
- **Early blight (*Alternaria solani*)** — yield losses commonly **30–60%**, up to **~79%** in severe studies (many non-US) (early-blight reviews, 2021).
- **Bacterial spot (*Xanthomonas* spp.)** — in **Florida** affects **~97% of acres**, up to **50% yield loss**, ~**$3,000/acre** field loss (MDPI *Agronomy*; PMC, 2020–2023).
- **Bacterial speck (*Pseudomonas syringae* pv. *tomato*)** — **20–25% seedling / up to 75% fruit** losses under outbreaks; no single US $ estimate found **[unverified]**.
- **Septoria leaf spot** — up to **~50%** (to 100% in heavily defoliated fields); US $ figure **[unverified]**.
- **Fusarium wilt** — typical **~14%**, up to **80%** in severe epidemics; soil-persistent; US $ figure **[unverified]**. **Verticillium wilt** — significant but no tomato-specific quantified figure found **[unverified]**.
- **Tomato yellow leaf curl virus (TYLCV)** — up to **100% yield loss**; in Florida (first detected 1997) intensive whitefly control holds losses **<~10%**, with damage **"in the tens of millions" in Florida** by the sources' own *imprecise estimate* (UGA / FL extension).
- **Tomato spotted wilt virus (TSWV)** — host range >1,000 species; **>$1 billion/yr worldwide**; **~$9 million/yr** in combined GA tomato+pepper losses; 75–100% losses reported in Hawaii (review literature; UGA).

### 4c. The 2009 Eastern-US late blight pandemic
- Spread when **infected tomato transplants were distributed via national big-box retailers** across the mid-Atlantic/Northeast; cool wet weather drove the **earliest widespread regional late blight ever observed**, via the aggressive **US-22** lineage (Fry et al., *Plant Disease*, 2013; APSnet).
- **Documented dollar figure:** **Bonnie Plants pulled >$1 million** of tomato plants from Northeast retail as a precaution (Greenhouse Grower, 2009). Many home gardeners/organic growers **lost entire crops**, and a US fungicide shortage occurred. A full epidemic-wide aggregate $ / acreage total was **not located. [unverified]**

### 4d. Why tomato is THE machine-learning / computer-vision prototyping crop
- **PlantVillage dataset** (Hughes & Salathé, 2015, **arXiv:1511.08060**): **54,306 images, 38 classes (crop-disease pairs), 14 crop species, 26 diseases** — and it includes tomato, apple, **and** cherry (Hughes & Salathé, 2015; Mohanty et al., 2016).
- **Tomato is the most disease-class-rich crop in PlantVillage — 10 classes** (9 diseases + healthy): Bacterial spot, Early blight, Late blight, Leaf Mold, Septoria leaf spot, Spider-mite/Two-spotted, Target Spot, Tomato mosaic virus, **Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus**, and Healthy (spMohanty/PlantVillage-Dataset GitHub; tomato-classification literature). Tomato also contributes a large image share (~14,500–18,160 images across the 10 classes in common redistributions) (Kaggle; *Sci. Reports*, 2025).
- **Benchmark:** Mohanty, Hughes & Salathé, *Frontiers in Plant Science* 7:1419 (2016) trained CNNs on the 38 classes to **99.35% accuracy** on held-out PlantVillage data — the seminal deep-learning-for-plant-disease paper.
- **Why tomato specifically:** (1) the **largest, most balanced set of distinct, visually-separable foliar disease classes** (10) for multi-class benchmarking; (2) a **globally and US-economically major crop** with well-characterized, photogenic leaf symptoms; (3) **abundant labeled images** allow training/ablation on one crop without leaving the dataset. *Practical takeaway for OrchardEye: tomato is the natural pretraining/prototyping crop, and the same dataset family already contains apple and cherry classes to transfer toward.*

## 5. WA-08 / Central Washington Tree-Fruit Context

### 5a. District composition (post-2020 redistricting; effective 2022)
- WA-08 **spans the Cascades**: eastern slices of **King, Pierce, Snohomish** on the west, crossing to include **Chelan and Kittitas counties in full**, plus **the East Wenatchee precinct in Douglas County** (Wikipedia; Ballotpedia, 2022). Eastside hubs: **Wenatchee, Leavenworth, Ellensburg** — the project's exact target geography. Current representative: **Rep. Kim Schrier (D)** (Ballotpedia, current).
- ⚠️ **Important nuance:** Of the big tree-fruit counties, **only Chelan and Kittitas are wholly in WA-08; Douglas is only partially in** (East Wenatchee). **Yakima, Grant, and Okanogan are NOT in WA-08** (they sit in WA-04 / WA-05). So WA-08 captures the **Wenatchee/Chelan/Leavenworth core** but not the southern (Yakima) or Columbia Basin (Grant) production zones.

### 5b. Concentration of tree fruit in central WA
- USDA NASS groups WA fruit into three districts: **Wenatchee (Chelan, Douglas, Okanogan)**, **Yakima Valley (Benton, Kittitas, Yakima)**, **Columbia Basin (Adams, Franklin, Grant)** (USDA NASS).
- **Wenatchee District (Chelan + Douglas + Okanogan — the WA-08 core):** produces ~**25% of WA apples**, ~**85% of WA winter pears** (leads North America in winter storage pears), and ~**45% of PNW sweet cherries** (WSU Extension, Chelan-Douglas).
  - **Chelan County ranks #1 in WA for pears, #2 for sweet cherries, #4 for apples**; crop acreage ~**apples 14,195 ac, pears 11,134 ac, sweet cherries 6,841 ac** (WSU Extension, Chelan-Douglas). Tree fruit is ~**97% of Chelan County's total ag market value** (the dollar split itself traces to a **2002** Census snapshot — dated, though the ~97% dominance still holds) (WSU Extension; USDA Census).
  - **Kittitas County** (in WA-08) sits in the Yakima Valley district but is a smaller producer than Yakima/Benton. **Kittitas-specific production figures: not found. [unverified]**
- Statewide, the three tree-fruit crops (apple, pear, sweet cherry) together exceed **~$2.8 billion farm-gate (~30% of state ag value)** (WSU / industry, 2022).

### 5c. WSU Tree Fruit Research & Extension Center (TFREC), Wenatchee
- **WSU's tree-fruit research-and-extension hub, located in Wenatchee** — "in the primary fruit-producing region of the world" — focused on **apple, pear, and cherry** (WSU TFREC).
- Established by the WA legislature **Feb 25, 1937** (the grower movement began in 1921); now ~**102 acres in Wenatchee + ~92 acres in Douglas County** (WSU TFREC History).
- Hosts ~8 WSU programs including **pests & diseases** and tree-fruit Extension, plus **USDA-ARS** plant-pathology/genetics scientists — directly relevant to disease detection (WSU; USDA-ARS).
- **New investment:** WSU is building an **$18 million, ~24,000 sq ft Plant Growth Facility** in Wenatchee (construction began April 2026; opening planned **summer 2027**) for **year-round disease research, environmental-stress, and root-health work** — strong institutional momentum behind tree-fruit disease science in the exact WA-08 geography OrchardEye targets (WSU Insider; Morning Ag Clips, 2026).
- Grower-facing home: **treefruit.wsu.edu**.

## 6. One-paragraph synthesis for the pitch
Plant disease destroys up to **40% of global crops** and costs **>$220 billion/yr** (FAO/IPPC, 2021), and **fruits/vegetables are the highest-loss category** (FAO). In OrchardEye's backyard the stakes are concrete: **Washington grows ~60–65% of US apples**, a **~$1.95–2.3 billion** crop (USDA NASS, 2024; WSTFA, 2025), and central WA's **Wenatchee district (Chelan/Douglas/Okanogan, the WA-08 core)** alone supplies ~**85% of WA winter pears** and ~**45% of PNW sweet cherries** (WSU Extension). The motivating disaster is **X-disease / Little Cherry Disease**, which forced removal of **238,856 cherry trees (~974 acres)** and cut grower revenue by **~$65 million (2015–2020)** at roughly **$118,095/acre** over a 7-year recovery (Molnar et al., *J. Extension*, 2022) — a disease with **no cure**, spread by **latent, symptomless carriers** detectable only by **molecular tests**, which is exactly the early-detection gap a fast, accessible scanner aims to narrow. Tomato anchors the ML side: the **PlantVillage** dataset (Hughes & Salathé, 2015) gives tomato **10 disease classes** and a path to **99.35%** CNN accuracy (Mohanty et al., 2016), with apple and cherry classes in the same dataset family to transfer toward.
